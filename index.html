Introduction to Accessible Images

As the publishing world shifts to electronic media, we have an unprecedented opportunity to ensure all content that is "born digital" is also "born accessible" so that all readers have access to the information they need. Digital images are still often accessible only to those who see well, but now there are many accessible alternatives for graphical content.

In many cases, a simple text description of an image will provide all the necessary information, but determining how much text is sufﬁcient depends on the audience and context of the image.

    In this training, you will:
  • review a collection of sample images to determine when a description is useful
  • review general guidelines and practice choosing the most appropriate description
  • practice writing descriptions for a variety of image types

Whether you are a professional publisher, content author, accessibility specialist, or a volunteer describer, you will ﬁnd resources to simplify and explain the image description process and ultimately make images accessible to all readers.

See examples of alternative ways to make images accessible at DIAGRAM Center's Accessible Image Sample Book.

Learn more about image accessibility by visiting the DIAGRAM Center.

When to Describe?

Does an image NEED to be described? That depends...

  • How important is the image for understanding the core content?
  • What is the purpose of the image - is it "eye candy" (As in this sunset) or associated with a function (like an icon)?
  • Is the image already adequately described in the surrounding text?
  • Is it better to provide a tactile graphic rendering of the image?

This section aims to guide you through the process of deciding when and how best to describe an image. You will be asked a series of upto nine questions that takes you through the key decision-making points involved in making this decision.
